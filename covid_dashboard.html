Key insights from the data
🧬
East Asia contained spread better
South Korea and Japan — despite having cases in the tens of millions — recorded CFRs of just 0.10% and 0.22%, far below the global median. Strong public health culture, masking compliance, and rapid test-trace-isolate systems likely drove this outcome.
CFR < 0.25%
📉
South America had the highest mortality burden
With a regional CFR of 1.96% — nearly double the global median — South America bore a disproportionate death toll. Brazil alone recorded 704K deaths from 38M cases, driven by healthcare system strain and delays in vaccine rollout during the Delta wave.
CFR 1.96% regional avg
💉
Higher vaccination ≠ lower case count
China (90% vacc.) and France (80%) still recorded ~100M and ~39M cases respectively. Vaccines reduced severity and death but didn't eliminate transmission — especially against Omicron variants. Oceania, with low CFR of 0.22%, benefited from late wave timing and high coverage.
Mortality reduction confirmed
🌍
Africa's numbers likely undercounted
Africa reported only 8.97M cases — just 1.15% of global total — despite housing 18% of the world's population. Limited testing infrastructure, younger demographics, and under-reporting suggest the true burden was substantially higher than official counts indicate.
Severe data gap
⚡
High-income countries had 3× more cases per capita
Wealthier nations had better testing infrastructure, which inflated case numbers. But they also had superior medical access, which kept CFRs lower. The US (1.2% CFR) vs. Brazil (1.85%) shows how healthcare capacity and timing of vaccine access drove mortality divergence even among high-case countries.
Infrastructure gap matters
🇮🇳
India ran the world's largest vaccine campaign
India administered doses to 952 million people — more than the entire population of any country except China. This unprecedented logistical achievement in a developing nation, using indigenously developed vaccines like Covaxin, set a global benchmark for mass immunisation at scale.
